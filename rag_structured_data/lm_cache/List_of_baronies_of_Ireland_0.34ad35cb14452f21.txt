Antrim’s baronies split into Lower and Upper pairs during 1792-1798: Antrim Lower stretches across 80,826 acres and Antrim Upper over 36,489, both named for Antrim town; Belfast Lower covers 56,142 acres and Belfast Upper 32,942, both echoing the town now city; Dunluce Lower holds 30,575 acres and Dunluce Upper 52,788, both linked to Dunluce Castle; Glenarm Lower extends 64,945 acres and Glenarm Upper 24,032, both named after Glenarm village; Toome Lower comprises 36,135 acres and Toome Upper 47,571, both taking their name from Toome village. Carrickfergus, recorded by 1325 and once the County of the Town of Carrickfergus, spans 16,702 acres. Cary, anglicised from Cathraí and known since 1672, covers 75,035 acres and recalls the ancient Cothrugu tribe. Kilconway, Coill Chonmaí in Irish, meaning “forest of the Conmhaícne,” embraces 68,640 acres. Massereene Lower, 27,228 acres, and Massereene Upper, 56,675 acres, both created 1792-1798, honour Viscount Massereene and a monastery once called “Queen’s hill.” Armagh barony, documented by 1609, occupies 47,645 acres and is named for Armagh town now city. Fews Lower, 29,757 acres, and Fews Upper, 47,433 acres, split by 1745 but Fews known since 1609, derive from “Na Feadha,” the lengths. Oneilland East, 20,890 acres, and Oneilland West, 57,584 acres, divided 1792-1807 yet Oneilland recorded by 1609, commemorate the Uí Nialláin tribe and are distinct from the O’Neills.
