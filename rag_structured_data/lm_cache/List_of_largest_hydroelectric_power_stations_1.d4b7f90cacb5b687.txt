China’s Jinsha River will host two of the world’s next giants: the 16,000 MW Baihetan Dam due 2021-2022 and the 10,200 MW Wudongde Dam due 2020-2021. Downstream on the Salween, Myanmar’s 7,110 MW TaSang project is on indefinite hold, while the 3,600 MW Myitsone on the Irrawaddy faces the same fate. Ethiopia is racing to finish the 6,450 MW Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam on the Blue Nile by 2022 and the 2,160 MW Koysha Dam on the Omo in 2021. Pakistan is pairing the 4,500 MW Diamer-Bhasha and the 4,320 MW Dasu, both on the Indus, for commissioning in 2023. Tajikistan’s 3,600 MW Rogun on the Vakhsh is scheduled across the decade ending 2029. Nigeria plans 3,050 MW from the Mambilla station on the Donga by 2024, while China’s 3,000 MW Lianghekou on the Yalong should spin up between 2021 and 2023. Colombia’s Ituango on the Cauca targets 2,456 MW, possibly in 2021, whereas Venezuela’s 2,320 MW Tocoma on the Caroní is stalled. Angola is building the 2,172 MW Caculo Cabaça for 2024 and expanding the 2,069.5 MW Lauca through 2020. China will add 2,200 MW with Maerdang on the Yellow River and 2,000 MW with Shuangjiangkou on the Dadu, while India’s 2,000 MW Subansiri Lower remains on hold.
