Brian Sonia-Wallace, who took his M.A. in 2011, is a poet, theatre maker and journalist who authored *I Sold These Poems, Now I Want Them Back*. Alistair Moffat, 1972 M.A., served as director of the Edinburgh Festival Fringe and later as Rector of his alma mater. The 1588 M.A. graduate Robert Aytoun combined poetry with law, becoming court poet to the queen of James I and VI and one of the first Scots to write in standard English. Andrew Crumey is a novelist and literary editor of *Scotland on Sunday*. Gavin Douglas, enrolled in 1494, became a bishop, makar and translator, while William Dunbar, M.A. 1479, earned fame as a poet and makar. Alexander Hume holds an M.A. and writes poetry. Robert Fergusson entered in 1763 but left without graduating, yet his verse strongly influenced Robert Burns. Sarah Hall, M.Litt., is a novelist-poet whose *The Electric Michaelangelo* was shortlisted for the Man Booker Prize. Gilbert Hay is known as a poet and translator. American James A. Michener studied as a Lippincott Fellow and won the Pulitzer Prize for Fiction. David Lyndsay served as Lord Lyon and wrote poetry. Bruce Marshall’s fiction and nonfiction have been repeatedly adapted for screen and television. Hilary McKay writes children’s books and won the 1992 Guardian Children’s Fiction Prize. Alastair Reynolds earned a PhD here before becoming a leading science-fiction author. William Tennant is a scholar-poet, Fay Weldon a feminist author-essayist-playwright, Timothy Williams took his M.A. in 1970 and later won a Crime Writers’ Association award, Andrew Lang collected folk tales while producing poetry, novels and criticism, and Robert Henryson stands as a celebrated poet and makar.
