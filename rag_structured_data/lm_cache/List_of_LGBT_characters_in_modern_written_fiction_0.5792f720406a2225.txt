Sheridan Le Fanu’s 1872 novella Carmilla features the eponymous Carmilla, a lesbian vampire who inaugurates the lesbian-vampire tradition. Alan Dale’s 1889 A Marriage Below Zero presents Arthur Ravener and Captain Dillington in what is hailed as the first English-language gay-themed novel, while Bayard Taylor’s 1870 Joseph and His Friend: A Story of Pennsylvania follows Joseph Asten and Philip Held in the first American gay-themed novel. Between 1835 and 1847 Honoré de Balzac introduces Jacques Collin, alias Vautrin or Trompe-la-Mort, across five volumes of La Comédie humaine, often cited as French literature’s first openly homosexual character. Oscar Wilde’s 1890 The Picture of Dorian Gray portrays the gay artist Basil Hallward and the bisexual Dorian Gray. The anonymous 1881 pornographic novel The Sins of the Cities of the Plain centers on Jack Saul, a male prostitute who cross-dresses and services men, one of the earliest English works devoted almost solely to homosexuality. Similarly, the anonymously published 1893 Teleny, or The Reverse of the Medal—attributed to Wilde—depicts the fervent affair between René Teleny and Camille de Grieux. Adolfo Caminha’s 1895 Bom-Crioulo rounds out the century with the gay character Amaro.
