Mercury’s Gainsborough crater, 95 km wide, honors English painter Thomas Gainsborough and was approved in 1985. Antoni Gaudí i Cornet, the Spanish Catalan architect, lent his name to the 81 km Gaudí crater in 2012, while French artist Paul Gauguin’s 70 km Gauguin crater dates to 1979. Irish stained-glass artist Wilhelmina Geddes is remembered in the 84 km Geddes crater, approved 2010. Lorenzo Ghiberti’s 110 km Ghiberti crater and Giotto di Bondone’s 144 km Giotto crater both received names in 1976. Giambologna, the Flemish sculptor, has the 69 km Giambologna crater approved 2013, and Lebanese-American poet-artist Kahlil Gibran the 106 km Gibran crater approved 2009. Russian composer Mikhail Glinka’s 89 km Glinka crater was named in 2008; Austrian composer Christoph Willibald Gluck’s 100 km Gluck crater in 1979. Johann Wolfgang von Goethe, the German writer, claims the 317.17 km Goethe basin, approved 1979. Russian playwright Nikolai Gogol’s 79 km Gogol crater was approved 1985, South African writer Nadine Gordimer the 58 km Gordimer crater in 2019. Spanish artist Francisco Goya’s 138 km Goya crater, Australian composer Percy Grainger’s 113 km Grainger crater, and Norwegian composer Edvard Grieg’s 59 km Grieg crater all trace to 1976, 2012, and 1985 respectively. Finnish-American ceramist Maija Grotell’s 48.25 km Grotell crater was approved 2012, and Italian music theorist Guido of Arezzo the 58 km Guido d’Arezzo crater in 1976.
