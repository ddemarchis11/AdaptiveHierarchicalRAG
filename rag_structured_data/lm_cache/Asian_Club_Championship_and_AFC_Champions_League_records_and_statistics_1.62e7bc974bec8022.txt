South Korea dominates the competition with 11 titles earned by six clubs: Pohang Steelers lead with three, Seongnam FC, Suwon Samsung Bluewings and Jeonbuk Hyundai Motors each have two, while Busan IPark and Ulsan Hyundai added one apiece; Seongnam FC and FC Seoul have each finished runner-up twice, Jeonbuk Hyundai Motors once and Yangzee once. Japan follows with seven trophies shared among Urawa Red Diamonds (two), JEF United Ichihara Chiba, Tokyo Verdy, Gamba Osaka, Júbilo Iwata and Kashima Antlers, while Júbilo Iwata have twice been runners-up and Yokohama F. Marinos and Urawa Red Diamonds once each. Saudi Arabia’s five wins come solely from Al-Hilal (three) and Al-Ittihad (two), yet the kingdom has produced nine runners-up: Al-Hilal four times, Al-Ahli twice, and Al-Ittihad, Al-Nassr and Al-Shabab once apiece. Iran has three titles via Esteghlal (two) and PAS Tehran (one) and five runners-up spots from Esteghlal (two), Sepahan, Zob Ahan and Persepolis (one each). China’s three wins are split between Guangzhou Evergrande (two) and Liaoning Whowin (one), with Liaoning Whowin and Dalian Wanda each finishing second once. Israel has three titles—Maccabi Tel Aviv (two) and Hapoel Tel Aviv (one)—and Hapoel Tel Aviv once runner-up. Qatar’s Al-Sadd have won twice and Al-Arabi been runners-up once; Thailand’s Thai Farmers Bank have two titles and BEC Tero Sasana one runners-up finish. The United Arab Emirates’ sole victory belongs to Al-Ain, who have also been runners-up twice, with Al-Ahli adding one second place. Australia’s Western Sydney Wanderers have one title and Adelaide United one runners-up finish. Iraq, Malaysia, Oman and Syria have each supplied two or one runners-up without yet claiming a title: Al-Shorta and Al-Rasheed for Iraq, Selangor for Malaysia, Oman Club for Oman, and Al-Karamah for Syria.
