The 661 km Yunnan-Guizhou Water Tunnel, due for completion in 2025, will dwarf every other conduit as an inter-river-basin water transfer in Yunnan, China. New York City Water Tunnel No. 3, finished in 2020, stretches 96.6 km to augment the city’s supply and, once fully linked, will rank third globally. Paris Metro’s circular Line 15, rolling out between 2022 and 2030, will ring the petite couronne for 75 km, overtaking Moscow’s 69 km Bolshaya Koltsevaya circular line completed in 2023. Barcelona’s L9/L10 metro reaches 43.7 km underground between 2009 and 2016, while Singapore’s 43 km Thomson-East Coast Line opens fully in 2024. The 36.7 km Woodsmith Mine Tunnel beneath England’s North York Moors, finished in 2021, conveys potash by conveyor. China’s 34.6 km Gaoligongshan rail tunnel, bored from 2017 to 2022, links Dali with Ruili. Austria’s 32.9 km Koralm Tunnel, 2025, and 27.3 km Semmering Base Tunnel, 2026, will speed Baltic-Adriatic trains, while the 55 km Brenner Base Tunnel, due 2028, will merge with the Inntal Tunnel to create a 64 km continuous rail tube under the Stubai Alps. Norway’s 26.7 km Rogfast road tunnel, under construction since 2018, will become both the longest and deepest undersea road tunnel at 392 m below sea level when it opens in 2026. Nepal’s 26 km Melamchi Water Tunnel, financed by the Asian Development Bank, has supplied Kathmandu since 2015. Thailand’s Mae Taeng-Mae Ngat and Mae Ngat-Mae Kuang water tunnels, 25.6 km and 23 km respectively, were completed in 2021. South Korea’s 25.1 km Musil Tunnel on the Jungang Line opened in 2018, Spain’s 24.7 km Pajares Base Tunnel awaits resolution of water-leak issues, Norway’s 19.5 km Follo Line started running in 2021, Sydney’s 19 km WestConnex road tunnel partially opened in 2019 and will finish in 2023, and Stockholm’s 16.5 km Förbifart road tunnel is scheduled for 2030.
