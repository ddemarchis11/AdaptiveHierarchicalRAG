Portland hosts the for-profit American College of Healthcare Sciences, founded 1978, enrolling 1,032 health-professions students, while the city’s Lutheran Concordia University, dating from 1905, educates 1,404 in master’s programs and Multnomah University, a 1936 Protestant religious school, counts 763. Salem counters with two Baptists: Corban University, established 1935, serving 754 master’s students, and Chemeketa Community College, opened 1970, teaching 9,228 associate-seekers. Public associates colleges dot the state: Pendleton’s Blue Mountain, 1962, enrolls 2,216; Bend’s Central Oregon, 1949, 4,048; Oregon City’s Clackamas, 1961, 6,902; Astoria’s Clatsop, 1958, 1,610; The Dalles’ Columbia Gorge, 1989, 831; Klamath Falls’ Klamath, 1996, 1,619; Eugene’s Lane, 1964, 9,110; Albany’s Linn-Benton, 1967, 5,391; Gresham’s Mt. Hood, 1965, 7,817. Newberg’s Quaker-founded George Fox University, 1891, is a 3,382-student research university, whereas Eugene’s tiny Gutenberg College, 1994 Protestant, enrolls only 48. McMinnville’s Linfield College, 1858 Baptist, and Portland’s Lewis & Clark, 1867, are liberal-arts colleges with 2,606 and 3,259 respectively. La Grande’s Eastern Oregon University, 1929, enrolls 3,338 master’s students, and St. Benedict’s Mount Angel Seminary, 1887 Catholic, educates 177.
