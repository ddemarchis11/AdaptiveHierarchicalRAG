Albert, the playful star of Eleanor May’s Mouse Math series, shares his adventures with sister Wanda, while Daniel Keyes’s Algernon is a laboratory mouse whose artificially boosted intelligence drives the tragedy of Flowers for Algernon. William Steig sends the seafaring mouse Amos into friendship with the whale Boris, and Eve Titus’s Anatole, a French mouse determined to clear his species’ name, first appeared in 1956. Katherine Holabird’s ballet-mad Angelina Mouseling twirls through mishaps alongside Alice, and Graham Oakley’s Arthur and Humphrey share church-yard escapades in Whortlethope with their feline ally Sampson. Robin Jarvis plunges Audrey Brown into Deptford’s sewers to battle an evil rat god, while Eve Titus also created Basil of Baker Street, the mouse detective who lives beneath Sherlock Holmes. Margery Sharp’s Bianca and Bernard belong to the Mouse Prisoner’s Aid Society, rescuing the distressed, and Julia Donaldson’s Big Bad Mouse terrifies predators with invented Gruffalo tales until the monster proves real. William Wise’s domestic explorer Christopher Mouse survives perilous travels, and Richard Peck’s Cranston family—Helena, Louise, Beatrice, Lamont, Vicky and Alice—navigate Secrets at Sea. Kate DiCamillo’s big-eared Despereaux Tilling, the sole survivor of his litter, is an outcast castle-mouse, while Douglas Adams’s Frankie and Benjy, hyper-intelligent mice, commissioned planet Earth. Edizioni Piemme’s jittery adventurer Geronimo Stilton chases emerald treasure, Beatrix Potter’s Hunca Munca and Tom Thumb wreck then repair a doll’s house, and Jane Breskin Zalben’s Jensen redecorates a museum with abstract art. Beatrix Potter also pairs the urbane Johnny Town-Mouse with country cousin Timmy Willie, each unnerved by the other’s home, while Brian Jacques’s Martin the Warrior escapes slavery, reclaims his father’s sword from Badrang the Tyrant, and saga-spans Mossflower and The Legend of Luke. Finally, Enid Blyton’s exiled Mary Mouse becomes maid to Sailor Doll in the dolls’ house.
