Emile Amblard’s circa-1900 guest house at 32-36 N. Vine St. in Waconia, listed on 4 January 1980, is the best-preserved remnant of the town’s resort era and the only surviving building designed by the promoter of Coney Island of the West, itself listed on 11 August 1976 as the 31-acre island ruins in Lake Waconia that drew crowds from the 1880s to the 1920s. In Chaska, the Brinkhaus Saloon Livery Barn of c. 1875 at 112 W. 4th St., entered 4 January 1980, now shelters the Chaska Historical Society, while nearby cream-colored Chaska brick defines the 1895 Albertine and Fred Heck House on Audubon Road in Chanhassen and the 1878 J. Carsten and Magaretha Harms Farmhouse off County Road 152 near Cologne. Frederick E. DuToit’s c. 1870 house at 121 Hickory St. in Chaska, listed the same day, honors the newspaper publisher and politician whose family shaped the city, as did the 1871 Herald Block at 123 W. 2nd St., long home to their Chaska Herald. Queen Anne elegance surfaces in the c. 1900 Eder-Baer House at 105 Elm St., while the c. 1870 Frederick Greiner House at 319 E. 3rd St. recalls an early hotelkeeper and public official. Cologne’s Philip Guettler House of 1902 at Adams and Mill Streets marks the second-generation owner of the town’s 1880 mill, and the adjacent 1905 John Knotz House belonged to a railroad doctor and his wife Rosa, three-time mayor and women’s-rights advocate. Victoria’s 1878 Laketown Moravian Brethren’s Church on County Highway 11 bridges plain early sanctuaries and later ornate brick churches, and the 1938 Chaska Historical Marker on County Highway 61 exemplifies National Park Service rustic style wayside rests. Off County Highway 11 in Carver Park Reserve, the 160-acre Wendelin Grimm Farmstead, listed 30 December 1974, preserves the 1876 house where Grimm developed winter-hardy alfalfa, now a Three Rivers educational facility. King Oscar’s Settlement on County Highway 40 near Carver holds an 1865 cottage, 1866 church, 1874 parsonage, and parish hall that birthed Gustavus Adolphus College within the first Swedish American settlement, while Hamburg’s 1907 Jacob Hebeisen Hardware Store on Railroad and Maria Streets became a 1919 consumer cooperative heralding Minnesota’s cooperative movement.
